Critter Clash

An educational card game designed for a real national client, taking Finnish biodiversity and turning it into something genuinely fun to play.

Year 2026
Role Game Designer, Systems Designer
Client Priodiversity LIFE Project
Context XAMK Class Project
The Brief
A real client, a real problem

Critter Clash started as a class project at XAMK, but it came with a real client brief from the Priodiversity LIFE Project, a national initiative running from 2024 to 2031 that is working to halt biodiversity loss in Finland.

The client needed something that could reach different audiences who do not usually engage with conservation topics. The brief called for an educational tool that could work for local town officials and landowners, for new experts being trained, and for ordinary citizens including younger people. Three very different audiences, one game.

Educating local officials and landowners about biodiversity decisions
Training new experts in local biodiversity knowledge
Teaching citizens and young people about their local nature

Design Process
From brief to playable prototype
Step 01
Brief Analysis and Ideation
After going through the brief and its limiting factors, I mapped out what the game needed to do before thinking about what it should be. My two reference points were the Pokemon Trading Card Game and Gwent: The Witcher Card Game. PTCG gave me the accessible, easy-to-follow structure. Gwent gave me the mechanical depth I wanted underneath it.
Step 02
Species Research
I selected two species from a provided list of Kymenlaakso habitats and did a focused research session on each. The goal was to find information I could actually use in the game mechanics, not just facts to display on a card.
Step 03
Prototype Creation
I designed a first set of prototype cards across three categories: Species, Weather Effects, and Resource Effects. All visuals at this stage were placeholders. The priority was getting something on the table that could be tested.
Step 04
Live Playtesting
I ran a hosted playtesting session with several players. The goal was not just collecting feedback but watching how people actually interacted with the game: where they got confused, what created moments of genuine engagement, and where the design was failing them.
Step 05
Iteration and Rebalancing
Based on what I observed and heard, I revised card descriptions, adjusted numbers, redesigned weather card effects, and refined the tags system. The game is still in active development with more testing rounds planned.

Card System
Three categories, one ecosystem
Category 01
Species Cards

The creatures of Kymenlaakso, reimagined as fantasy-inspired characters. They are played onto the field and have strengths and weaknesses relative to each other, which creates genuine incentive to think about deck composition.

Category 02
Weather Effects

Cards that affect the entire field simultaneously. These started underpowered and were adjusted after playtesting to offer meaningful positives with slight negatives, making them worth including in a deck rather than always being passed over for stronger species.

Category 03
Resource Effects

Cards that modify the rules and interactions between players. These affect game flow rather than field strength, adding a layer of strategic planning around when to play them and how they shift the dynamic of a round.

Core Mechanics
Easy to learn, not easy to master

Players build a 50-card deck and take turns playing species onto the field to create an ecosystem. Each species has tags and abilities that interact with other species, creating synergies and conflicts that reward players who pay attention to how species relate to each other in real ecology.

After both players take a turn, the total strength of all species on the field is calculated and converted into Stability points. The first player to reach 30 Stability wins. The education is built into the mechanics themselves; players absorb information about species relationships by playing the game, not by reading fact sheets.

What makes it distinct
Ecology as strategy, not lecture

Most educational card games make the learning feel like a tax on the fun. Critter Clash tries to do the opposite: the ecological relationships are the strategy. Pollinators interact with flowering plants. Predators affect prey species. The theme of Kymenlaakso's ecosystems makes mechanics intuitive rather than arbitrary.

The result is a game that works at two levels. Players who know nothing about biodiversity can play and enjoy it. Players who know their Finnish species will recognise things and get something extra from the experience.

Playtesting Results
What changed and why
Card Iteration: Lullula Arborea (Woodlark)
Before

Base strength felt weak compared to most other species. The card's conditional ability required other birds to be present, but it was the only bird in the base deck, making the condition nearly impossible to trigger in practice.

After

Buffed base strength to make the card worth including even without triggering the condition. Added coloured tags for easier reading and comparison. The card now has a clear role in the deck rather than just being filler.

System Iteration: Weather Cards
Before

Playtesters consistently chose species cards with 3+ strength over weather cards. The weather effects were too minimal to justify the deck slot. Nobody was including them voluntarily.

After

Rebalanced to give weather cards larger positive effects with slight negatives. This creates a real decision point: weather cards now carry strategic value and risk, making them worth thinking about during deckbuilding.
